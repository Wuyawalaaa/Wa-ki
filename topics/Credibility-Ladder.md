# Credibility Ladder

> A six-rung progression for how a public-facing operator earns increasing levels of credibility in a research / discourse community — each rung gates the actions available at the next.

## The ladder

【事实】[CI]

| Rung | Action | What it earns |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Zero presence | — |
| 1 | Substantive comments on others' research | "This person knows the space" |
| 2 | Original analysis threads | "This person has original ideas" |
| 3 | Cited / retweeted by established researchers | "This person is peer-validated" |
| 4 | Invites others to engage with their work | "This person leads a discourse" |
| 5 | Formal research program / fellowship | "This person is an institution" |

## How to read it

【推断】Each rung is **gated** by the rungs below — you can't successfully run a Rung-5 fellowship if no one at Rungs 1–3 has heard of you, because the people you'd want as fellows don't show up. The Eigenlayer Research Fellowship works because Sreeram Kannan was already at Rung 3 (peer-validated, cited) before running it.

【推断】The progression is also **what each rung produces**, not just what it requires. Rung 1 actions are not a step you graduate from — they continue at every higher rung. A Rung 4 operator still does Rung 1 work; what changes is the additional surface they've added on top.

【推断】The hardest transition is Rung 0 → Rung 1, because Rung 0 by definition has no audience to convert. This is the cold-start problem: substantive comments are visible only inside someone else's audience, so the path off Rung 0 runs through *other people's* attention rather than your own. [[Reply-Fishing]] is the named tactic for this transition; it does not apply at higher rungs because higher rungs already have surface area.

## Failure modes

【推断】**Skipping rungs.** Trying to run Rung-5 actions (formal program) from Rung 1 (some good comments) reads as performative and burns the standing accumulated at Rung 1. The ladder's order is a constraint, not a suggestion.

【推断】**Misidentifying current rung.** Self-assessment of where on the ladder you actually sit is the most common error — most operators believe themselves a rung higher than the discourse community treats them. Calibration comes from observing whether higher-rung operators *cite* you (Rung 3 evidence) versus whether they merely respond to you (Rung 1–2).

【推断】**Performing the rung instead of doing it.** Rung 4 is "invites others to engage with their work" — a real Rung 4 operator has work substantial enough that engagement is its own reward for the inviter. A performed Rung 4 (running a calls-for-feedback program with no underlying body of work) collapses on first contact.
