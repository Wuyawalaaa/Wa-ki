# Category Design

> The strategy of coining a category-defining term and embedding it into discourse so that you become the implicit canonical reference for an entire space — projects describing themselves in your vocabulary are doing your marketing for you.

## Core insight

【推断】Being first beats being better when everyone remembers who started the conversation. Projects that create entirely new narratives become the reference point for that whole category. When you coin a term and others adopt it, future projects describing themselves in your vocabulary are doing your marketing for you. [CI]

## Worked examples

【事实】[CI]

| Project | Term coined | What it became |
|---|---|---|
| Kaito | "InfoFi" | Category label for crypto-information markets |
| Hivemapper | "Drive to Earn" | DePIN sub-category — others now describe themselves in this frame |
| Ondo | "RWA tokenization leader" | Self-positioning that became the canonical RWA shorthand |

These projects didn't have categorically better products than every competitor. They had earlier *vocabulary*, which compounded into being the reference point.

## Mechanism

【推断】Three compounding effects:

1. **Discourse infrastructure.** When a term enters discourse, every subsequent conversation about the topic uses your vocabulary. You become invisible plumbing — like coining "the cloud" for distributed compute, you're built into how people *think* about the space.

2. **Self-marketing through citation.** New entrants describing themselves in your vocabulary are advertising your category-leadership for free. Each citation is a passive endorsement of your framing.

3. **AI-corpus capture.** [[Generative-Engine-Optimization]] — once your term is in published material, AI training corpora ingest the term's definition with your project as the implicit canonical reference. When a user asks an AI "what is X?" and X is your coined term, the AI's answer is shaped by what you wrote.

## Decision: coin or don't?

【推断】Not every project should attempt category design. The framework applies when:

| Condition | Why |
|---|---|
| The category genuinely doesn't have a term yet | Otherwise you're displacing an existing term — much harder |
| Your project is structurally distinct, not just a feature variant | A term for "yet another competitor" doesn't compound; a term for a real new shape does |
| You have the patience to use the term consistently for months / years | Sporadic use of the term fragments the signal — if you sometimes call it X and sometimes call it Y, AI corpora can't link them |
| You're early enough in the category's life-cycle | After 2-3 competing terms exist, the window has closed |

When these don't hold, attempting category design produces wasted effort — you adopt vocabulary that nobody picks up, and you've reduced clarity by inventing terms instead of using established ones.

## How to actually coin a term

【推断】Three disciplines:

1. **Use it in every piece of content from day one.** The term has to appear thousands of times across your output before it becomes infrastructure. Inconsistent use ("we sometimes call this X, sometimes Y") fragments the signal.

2. **Define it in writing that lasts.** A whitepaper / paper / canonical post that defines the term becomes the citable reference. Ad-hoc uses on social don't accumulate the same way — they get cited but not anchored.

3. **Don't get protective.** When others use the term *without crediting you*, that's not a failure — it's the goal. The term has become discourse infrastructure. Attempting to control / trademark / police usage actively undermines category leadership; the term should feel like common vocabulary.

## What success looks like

【推断】The success signal is **the term spreading independently of you**. Strangers using "InfoFi" or "Drive to Earn" without mentioning the originating project means the term has graduated into discourse — which is exactly when you've won, even though it stops feeling like marketing.

## Branding vs. category terms

【推断】Two different terminology questions, often conflated: [CI]

| Type | Example | What compounds |
|---|---|---|
| Project name | "Kaito", "Hivemapper", "Ondo" | Brand recognition for *your project* specifically |
| Category term | "InfoFi", "Drive to Earn", "RWA tokenization" | Recognition for an *entire category* with you as the implicit reference |

Category terms have higher GEO compounding power than project names — projects come and go, but categories persist for years. A project that owns its category term has a moat that survives competitors entering; a project with only a strong brand name has no equivalent moat.

## Failure modes

【推断】**Inventing a term nobody adopts.** Most attempted category design fails — the coined term doesn't spread. Sign of this: after six months, you're the only one using the term. The term wasn't capturing anything real, or wasn't catchier than existing alternatives. Drop it; don't double down.

【推断】**Multiple competing terms across own surfaces.** Inventory's framing: if you use "stochastic settlement" on X, "probabilistic finality" on Reddit, and "random settlement" in a Mirror post, AI corpora can't link them. Three half-attempts at category design produce zero successful one. Pick one term and use it consistently. [CI]

【推断】**Optimizing the project name when the category term matters more.** Founders often spend disproportionate effort on project naming and branding while neglecting the category term — even though the category term, if it spreads, has 10×+ the long-term value.
