# Essays vs Articles

> Dan Koe's argument that **essays** (writing as thinking-in-progress) and **articles** (writing as packaged conclusion) are categorically different artifacts — and that AI can produce the latter but not the former. Frames the difference as the basis of a coming "meaning economy."

## Source layering

This page summarizes Dan Koe's recent essay as paraphrased by a Chinese-language synthesis [WX]. The framing is Koe's; the five-distinction table is the synthesizer's organization. Treat as Koe's argument-as-summarized rather than verbatim.

## The "pseudo-thinking" claim

【事实】Short videos, trending topics, and AI summaries deliver **conclusions while skipping the thinking process**. The argument: this produces an audience trained to recognize answers but not to construct them. [WX]

【事实】Koe invokes the metaphor of an **epistemic commons** — like a water source. Pollution without purification contaminates shared knowledge. [WX]

【推断】The "epistemic commons" framing is not new to Koe — it's standard in epistemology and was popularized in this exact form by writers like C. Thi Nguyen. Koe's contribution is applying it specifically to short-form / AI-summary consumption habits, framing them as the pollutant rather than as neutral information channels.

## Five distinctions between articles and essays

【事实】[WX]

| Articles | Essays |
|---|---|
| Provide answers | Build arguments |
| Package existing knowledge | Transform beliefs |
| Start with conclusions | Discover conclusions through writing |
| Inform | Constitute thinking itself |
| Convey the known | Uncover the unknown |

## The AI limitation claim

【事实】Koe's central claim: AI cannot replicate the human capacity for a **"situated point of view"** or the transformative moment when a writer realizes mid-composition that they were wrong. [WX]

【推断】This is a normative position, not an empirical finding about LLM capability. The argument doesn't depend on AI being unable to *output* essay-shaped text — current models clearly can. It depends on whether *the act of writing changing the writer's beliefs* is the load-bearing function. If yes, the artifact is incidental and AI's output is irrelevant; if the artifact alone matters, the position is harder to defend.

【推断】The strongest version of Koe's claim is process-centered: essays are valuable as **a thinking method**, not as content. By that framing, "AI writes my essay" is a category error — like "AI does my workout." The output exists, the function does not. Whether readers care about that distinction is the open question Koe doesn't resolve.

## The "meaning economy" thesis

【事实】[WX]

> "Meaning represents civilization's scarcest commodity."

| | Fast content | Slow content |
|---|---|---|
| Posture | Pre-digested conclusions | Collaborative thinking |
| Output | Hollow knowledge | Authentic significance |
| Cognitive role | Replaces thinking | Generates thinking |

## Where the argument lands

【推断】Koe's pitch is to creators: **write essays, not articles** — because articles are commoditized by AI and essays aren't. The strategic implication for solo operators: an essay practice is a moat in a way an article practice no longer is. This connects to [[Dan-Koe]]'s broader thesis that **the durable creator asset is the operator's POV**, not their output volume — articles scale volume, essays scale the POV.

【推断】The "fast content vs slow content" dichotomy is a content-domain analog of [[Slow-AI]]'s "fast AI vs slow AI" distinction in product design. Different domains, parallel insight: the new value is in the work that takes longer than the medium's default speed allows.

## Limits of the framing

【推断】The article/essay binary is sharper than reality. Most published writing sits in the middle — argument-driven enough to require thinking, packaged enough to read like an article. The binary is rhetorically useful but operationally fuzzy: the question "is this an article or an essay?" is less productive than "did the author change their mind while writing?"

【推断】The thesis is also vulnerable to a population argument: if essay-as-thinking matters mainly to the writer, the writer doesn't need readers to have one — but Koe is *publishing* the essay-about-essays. The framing rests on essays being valuable to read, which reintroduces the artifact view he tried to dismiss.
