# Physics-Based Communications

> Lulu Cheng Meservey's framework using the physics formula **P = F / A** (pressure = force ÷ area) to decide whether to spread or concentrate communications force depending on whether you're playing offense or defense.

## The formula

【事实】**P = F / A** — pressure equals force divided by surface area. [LM]

## Defense vs. offense

【事实】[LM]

| Posture | Action | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Under attack (defense) | **Spread** the force | Distributing across many surfaces dilutes the pressure on any single point — no single weakness gets crushed |
| Attacking (offense) | **Concentrate** the force | All force at one point produces maximum pressure — the small surface area is what makes the wedge drive in |

The same physical formula yields opposite advice depending on which side you're playing.

## How to apply it

### Defending

【推断】When you're getting hit — by a critic, a competitor, a hostile media cycle — the instinct is to push back hard at the attacking source. The framework says: don't. Instead, distribute the response across many surfaces simultaneously (multiple supportive voices, multiple platforms, multiple framings) so no single rebuttal is the focal point. The audience can't tell where the "real" response is, and the pressure on any one of your defenses is low. [CI, LM]

### Attacking

【推断】When you're trying to break into a crowded space — zero-follower founder pushing into established discourse, small project trying to claim a category — spreading effort across 5 platforms × 5 audience segments × 5 messages produces five-times-zero. The framework says: pick **one platform, one audience, one message** and push hard at a single point. The wedge drives in because the surface area is small. Expansion happens *after* the wedge is in, never before. [CI]

## Why this is a useful primitive

【推断】Most comms decisions reduce to the same two questions: "where do I put my limited time/attention?" and "how do I hit the target?" P = F / A formalizes the trade-off — you can't have both *coverage* and *impact* on a fixed force budget. The framework makes the choice explicit instead of implicit.

【推断】The model also explains why generic "be on every platform" advice fails for early-stage operators. Spreading across platforms increases A; with F bounded by founder time, P drops below the threshold needed to register on any single platform. The result is the "invisible everywhere" failure mode.

## Diagnostic use

【推断】Asked of any specific comms move:

1. Am I currently the attacker or the defender in this situation?
2. If attacker: what's the *one* point I'm concentrating force at? (If the answer is "many" — F is too thinly distributed)
3. If defender: am I creating one bullseye or distributing the surface? (If "one" — easy target)

## Failure modes

【推断】**Attacker behaving like defender.** The most common error among early founders. They want to be visible everywhere, so they spread thin. Result: high reach, zero depth, no wedge.

【推断】**Defender behaving like attacker.** A founder under fire who concentrates response on the attacker (replies, threads, rebuttals to one critic) creates a focal point that channels more attention to the attack. Spreading into supportive surfaces around the attack — without engaging the attacker directly — is more effective.

【推断】**Misidentifying posture.** Some situations look like attack opportunities but are actually defense (e.g., responding to a misinterpretation of your idea). Some look like defense but are actually offense (e.g., a critic creating an opening for a clarifying broadside). The diagnostic question — am I attacker or defender here? — is itself the hardest part.
