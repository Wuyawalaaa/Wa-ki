# Concentric Circles

> Lulu Cheng Meservey's framework for spreading a message: identify the smallest, most passionate core audience first, then expand outward ring by ring — never address all audiences at once.

## Origin

【事实】Attributed to Lulu Cheng Meservey (former VP Comms at Substack, EVP Corporate Affairs at Activision Blizzard, founder of Rostra). She discusses the framework directly on the Lenny Rachitsky podcast at timestamps 20:25 ("How Substack used the concentric circles framework to spread information") and 21:32 ("Understanding the layers in those concentric circles"). [LP]

【推断】Lulu's broader communications doctrine — that founders should bypass traditional PR intermediaries and speak directly to their audience — is laid out in her [[Go-Direct]] manifesto. Concentric Circles is the *targeting* layer of that doctrine; Go Direct is the *posture*. The framework answers "speak directly to whom, in what order?" given that you've already accepted you should speak directly. [GD]

## Core principle

【推断】Identify your audience by starting with the smallest, most passionate core and expanding outward. Don't try to address everyone at once. [CI]

【事实】Buteau's synthesis names a sequence: "self → co-founders → employees → investors → power users — expanding outward in sequence." [AB]

【推断】**Caveat on the exact ring list.** Buteau's sequence describes the *internal* application of the framework (how a founder's message propagates through their own concentric organization). The framework also runs *externally*: targeting the smallest, most discerning *external* audience first, then letting their adoption pull broader audiences in. The two applications are recursive — same shape, different scope — and the specific ring labels depend on which scope you're operating in. [CI, AB]

## Mechanism

【推断】Each ring's adoption creates social proof that pulls the next ring inward. The inner ring's belief is what makes the next ring's belief rational; you can't manufacture social proof from outside the rings. [CI]

【推断】**Skip rings and you lose the cascade.** Trying to address Ring 4 directly without Ring 1's validation produces a message that reads as unsupported assertion — the audience has no third-party signal that this matters, so the message slides past. [CI]

【推断】**Why the smallest audience first.** A small, passionate audience can be reached with high signal density (DMs, in-person, customized framing). The cost-per-conversion is high but the per-conversion *value* is also high — these people will repeat your message, validate it to outsiders, defend it under pushback. Larger audiences are reachable only at low signal density, and low-density messages need pre-existing credibility to land — which is exactly what Ring 1 generates.

## Why the framework holds

【事实】The supporting principle from Lulu (via Buteau, on the related "Cultural Erogenous Zones" framework): audiences have settled preferences. "It's a huge lift to try to change someone's worldview or their passions. It's a light lift to take the thing you want to talk about and just shape it into to fit into their worldview or their passions." [AB]

【推断】Concentric Circles operationalizes the same principle at the targeting layer: pick the audience whose existing worldview is *closest to* the message you carry, and reach them first. The cascade outward works because each successive ring's existing worldview is one degree further from the message — but the prior ring's adoption supplies the social proof that closes the gap.

## Failure modes

【推断】**Premature broadening.** A common error is treating Ring 1 as a stepping-stone to be exited as fast as possible. Ring 1 is not a phase — it remains the credibility source forever. Operators who "graduate" from Ring 1 into Ring 4 lose the validation engine and become vulnerable to any well-resourced challenger.

【推断】**Diluting the core message to widen the funnel.** When a message is broadened to include Rings 2–3 simultaneously, the framing becomes less specific to Ring 1 and Ring 1 disengages. The cascade collapses from both ends.
