## Aakash's actual framing — "What's the ride equivalent?"

The framework is not "imitation" — it is a *reframing question*. When most fintech companies launch a referral program, they copy Amazon or Uber's mechanics ($20 for $20) literally and call it done. Aakash's question forces a step back: what is the *psychological equivalent* of "a ride" for your product? For Uber, it's the ride. For Robinhood, it's the stock — not cash.

> "Most fintech companies launch a referral program and call it done. They copy what Amazon or Uber did — maybe $20 for $20 — and move on."

The lesson: do not imitate competitor mechanics. Identify your category's core emotional/functional unit, then obsess over execution within constraints.

## Robinhood referral program — 60+ tests

| Stage | Reward | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cash ($10) | Worked, but conversion insufficient |
| 2 | Variable cash amounts | Better, still suboptimal |
| 3 | **Variable stocks** | Significant improvement — the winning version |

**The critical discovery:** customers were *not* sensitive to expected monetary value. They cared about two things:

1. Receiving an actual stock (not cash)
2. The chance of receiving a really valuable stock

> "Customers actually weren't that sensitive to the expected value. What they really cared about was twofold: that they were getting a stock and that they had some chance of getting a really valuable stock."

## Activation friction insight

Robinhood deliberately added friction: users must *affirmatively claim* the stock. The act of claiming triggers ownership mentality. Then they layered price-movement and news notifications to deepen engagement.

This is friction-as-feature: not removing every step, but adding the right step that converts a passive recipient into an owner.

## Aakash's named principles (cross-cutting Robinhood lessons)

| # | Principle | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | UX as product | Pixel-level attention required; experience polish IS competitive advantage |
| 2 | Swipies-first design | Write product onboarding screens BEFORE building the feature — forces ruthless clarity |
| 3 | AI as information tool, not decision engine | Robinhood's Cortex provides context (news, data, research) without recommendations — respects regulatory constraints |
| 4 | Legal as strategic partner | Compliance teams treated as domain-expert collaborators, not blockers |
| 5 | Value-first pricing | Price for customer benefit within regulatory bounds, not profit maximization |

## Direct quotes

> "If you can't convince a customer to hit 'Get Started' in one sentence, you don't have a great product."
> — On the swipies-first design discipline.

> "When you have a legal partner or compliance partner who's bought in to the vision of the product, the outcomes of that product are so much better."
> — On productizing compliance.

## Other product examples Aakash discusses

- **Cortex (AI assistant)** — addresses the "why did my stock move 5%?" pain point. Combines licensed news, research, market data, SEC filings. *Deliberately avoids investment recommendations* to stay below the regulatory bar that would trigger broker-dealer obligations.
- **IPO access feature** — swipies tagline: "Get in at the IPO price." Directly addressed the customer pain of missing early trading windows.
- **Credit cards & banking expansion** — same thesis: democratize access, maximize customer value.

## Core thesis (Aakash's, in his own framing)

Don't imitate competitor mechanics. Identify your category's core emotional/functional need ("the ride equivalent"), then obsess over execution and customer value delivery within constraints.
